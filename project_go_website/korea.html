The Game of Go: Culture & History

Go is a type of strategic board game played with a gridded board and black and white stones. It originated in China and was known by various names such as "yi," "qi," and "shuotan" in ancient China, constituting one of the Four Arts alongside music, chess, and calligraphy.

The Korea Baduk Association (Korean: 한국기원) is the governing body for professional Go in South Korea, with its origins tracing back to the founding of the Hancheng Go Institute by Cho Namchul in November 1945.

In 1937, Cho Namchul went to Japan to study under the ninth-dan Go player Kitani Minoru. He returned to Korea in 1945 and established the Go Institute. Traditionally, Koreans used the "Xunjiang" opening strategy, but after the establishment of the Korea Baduk Association, Cho persuaded Koreans to adopt the "Bai Ban" opening strategy. In 1956, he founded the National Handicap Go Championship, popularizing Go competitions among the general public.

Famous professional Go players:

Cho Namchul

Cho Namchul (Korean: 조남철, November 30, 1923 – July 2, 2006), was a South Korean professional Go player of the ninth dan rank, widely regarded as the father of modern Go in Korea.

In 1946, Cho Namchul founded the Hancheng Go Institute, marking the official establishment of professional Go in South Korea. In 1950, under Cho Namchul's organization, South Korea held its first modern Go ranking tournament, with Cho being ranked as 3-dan and others being ranked as 1-dan. In 1955, Cho persuaded South Korean President Lee Seungman to organize a delegation of professional Go players to Taiwan, where they engaged in international matches against local Go players and emerged victorious. This victory lifted the spirits of the South Korean people and brought Cho and modern Go to prominence. Soon after, professional Go tournaments were established in South Korea, and the Hancheng Go Institute was rebuilt in 1968, renamed as the Korea Baduk Association. Cho himself dominated the South Korean Go scene for many years after the 1950s until his influence waned upon the return of his junior, Go player Kim In. He was awarded the rank of 9-dan in 1983 and in 1994, at an advanced age, he competed in the National Go Championship, becoming the oldest player to compete in the professional Go tournament. His nephew, Cho Chihyun, studied in Japan with his support and later became a prominent figure in the Japanese Go community.

Cao Xunxuan

Cao Xunxuan (Korean: 조훈현, born March 10, 1953) is a renowned Go player from South Korea. He holds the rank of professional 9-dan in the Korea Baduk Association and has won the World Go Championship nine times. Cho is considered one of the founding figures of modern Go in South Korea and is revered as the "Emperor of Go."

Cao Xunxuan is the master of Lee Changho, the record holder of the World Go Championship. They are known as the "Cao-Lee Master-Disciple". Along with Lee Changho, Liu Changhe, and Xu Fengzhu, they are collectively referred to as the "Four Heavenly Kings of South Korea," dominating the world Go scene from the 1990s to the mid-2000s. Cao was born in Mokpo, South Jeolla Province. He became a professional Go player at the youngest age in the history of the Korea Baduk Association at the age of 9 years and 7 months in 1962. In 1963, Cao went to Japan to study Go and became a disciple of Segoe Kensaku. He re-entered the professional Go ranks in Japan and was recognized as a 5-dan by the Nihon Ki-in in 1972. However, he returned to Korea due to his military service and developed a close friendship with the famous amateur Go player and South Korea's gambling king, Cha Minsu, during his military service. After completing his military service, Cao originally planned to return to Japan, but he chose to stay in Korea after facing intense competition from Xu Fengzhu. Cao emerged victorious in the years-long rivalry with Xu Fengzhu and became the dominant figure in the South Korean Go scene. He won over 130 domestic Go championships in South Korea and was promoted to 9-dan in 1982. In 1989, Cao defeated Nie Weiping, the Chinese Go master, 3-2 in the first Ing Cup competition, winning the championship and igniting a Go craze in South Korea, becoming a national hero.

Lee Changho

Lee Changho (Korean: 이창호, born July 29, 1975), is a Go player born in Jeonju, North Jeolla Province, South Korea. He is considered the second-generation master of Go in South Korea and is one of the greatest Go players in modern Go history, second only to Go Seigen. He is often referred to as "In the 20th century, there was Wu Qingyuan, and in the 21st century, there is Lee Changho."

In 1986, at the age of 11, Lee Changho achieved professional rank, second only to Cao Xunxuan's 9-year-old achievement. In 1992, at the age of less than 16 years and 6 months, he won the world championship, making him the youngest world Go champion to date. In 1996, the Korea Baduk Association granted him and Liu Changhe direct promotion from 7-dan to 9-dan. Lee has won all domestic championships in South Korea and achieved the Grand Slam of world Go tournaments. His active years in the international Go scene spanned from 1992 to 2012, with the period from 1996 to 2006 being recognized as the "Lee Changho era" in the Go world. Throughout his professional career, Lee has won 17 world championships and 21 international tournament titles, still holding the world number one ranking. Lee Changho is not only a champion in individual competitions but also a hero in team competitions. With his unwavering determination, he achieved 14 consecutive victories in the Three Nations Cup, ended the match 9 times, with a total record of 29 wins and 7 losses. He is revered by South Korean Go fans as the "guardian deity" and is known as the "Iron Gate" by Go enthusiasts in China and Japan.

Lee Changho, along with Cao Xunxuan, Xu Fengzhu, and Liu Changhe, are known as the "Four Heavenly Kings" of South Korea. Meanwhile, Lee Sedol, Park Yeonghun, and Choi Cheolhan are regarded as the "New Four Heavenly Kings." Due to similarities in age, pronunciation of names, domestic achievements, and playing style with China's Chang Hao, both Lee Changho and Chang Hao are referred to as the "Peerless Pair." However, in terms of international achievements and head-to-head records, Chang Hao falls behind significantly.

Lee Sedol

Lee Sedol (Korean: 이세돌, born March 2, 1983) is a South Korean Go player holding the rank of 9-dan. He grew up in Byeokje Island, Sinan County, South Jeolla Province, South Korea, earning him the nickname "Boy from Byeokje Island." He is also known as the "Invincible Boy" due to his impressive early career performances in domestic competitions in South Korea. Lee Sedol is considered the third-generation master of Go in South Korea. Alongside Lee Changho, he is referred to as "Big and Small Lee." Together with Lee Changho, Park Yeonghun, and Choi Cheolhan, Lee Sedol is recognized as one of the "New Four Heavenly Kings" of South Korea. He is also compared to China's Gu Li, being called the "Peerless Pair." Throughout his career, Lee Sedol has won 14 world championships and 18 international tournament titles, second only to Lee Changho, making him one of the most successful Go players in history. His peak performance was from 2002 to 2012.

In his early career, Lee Sedol's playing style was sharp and aggressive, excelling in large-scale attacks, earning him the nickname "Fourteen-dan" in the middle game, but his opening moves were relatively weak. As he matured and his skills developed, he transformed into a versatile player, capable of both offense and defense, becoming a master of the Go world. His playing style became subtle and deceptive, often employing cunning moves and displaying precise calculation. He would frequently sacrifice large groups of stones in the opening, only to resurrect them later using various strategic maneuvers, a style affectionately known as the "zombie flow" among Go enthusiasts. Lee Sedol also possessed exceptional psychological resilience, often turning the tables and clinching victory even when trailing in world championship matches. From 2005 to 2010, he engaged in multiple showdowns with China's Gu Li, drawing significant attention from the Go community as their match records remained evenly matched, establishing them as contemporaneous rivals. In 2016, Lee Sedol faced off against Google's AlphaGo AI software in a historic five-game match. Although he ultimately lost the series 1-4, his victory in one game demonstrated that humans still possessed the potential to defeat computers in the game of Go.
