The Game of Go: Culture & History

Go is a type of strategic board game played with a gridded board and black and white stones. It originated in China and was known by various names such as "yi," "qi," and "shuotan" in ancient China, constituting one of the Four Arts alongside music, chess, and calligraphy.

The Nihon Ki-in (Japanese: 一般公益法人日本棋院, Ippankōekihōjin Nihon kiin) is the largest Go organization in Japan, responsible for managing various Go tournaments. Its headquarters is located in Chiyoda Ward, Tokyo, with additional branch offices in Nagoya and Osaka, serving as central and Kansai headquarters respectively. Currently, the Nihon Ki-in oversees approximately 300 Go players.

The Nihon Ki-in was established in 1924 through the merger of the two largest Go organizations at the time, Honinbo Mon and Hogensha, with the support of Okura Kishichiro. After its establishment, the Nihon Ki-in faced competition from other organizations and individuals such as Shigematsu Shigeo, Wu Qingyuan (Go Seigen), and Weiqi Shinsha. In 1950, a group of Go players led by Hashimoto Utaro broke away from the Nihon Ki-in and founded the Kansai Ki-in, establishing their own organization in the Kansai region.

The Nihon Ki-in Title Matches

The seven major Go tournaments in Japan, ranked by prize money, are Kisei, Meijin, Honinbo, Oza, Tengen, Gosei, and Judan. Among these, the top three tournaments in terms of prize money, Kisei, Meijin, and Honinbo, are collectively known as the "Three Major Title Matches." Among them, the Kisei is the highest title. If a player concurrently holds all three titles, they are referred to as achieving the "Big Triple Crown."

The most significant feature of the seven major Go tournaments is that all Go players have the opportunity to participate. However, the methods for determining challengers vary. In the Meijin and Honinbo tournaments, players enter a preliminary round before advancing to the league stage. Entering the league stage is a testament to a player's status as a top-tier Go player.

The prize money for the title matches is as follows:

  • Kisei: 45,000,000 Japanese Yen
  • Judan: 7,000,000 Japanese Yen
  • Honinbo: 28,000,000 Japanese Yen
  • Gosei: 8,000,000 Japanese Yen
  • Meijin: 30,000,000 Japanese Yen
  • Oza: 14,000,000 Japanese Yen
  • Tengen: 13,000,000 Japanese Yen
