The Game of Go: Culture & History

Go is a type of strategic board game played with a gridded board and black and white stones. It originated in China and was known by various names such as "yi," "qi," and "shuotan" in ancient China, constituting one of the Four Arts alongside music, chess, and calligraphy.

The Nature of Go

Active period: From the Zhou Dynasty to the Present
Type: Board Game
Number of Players: Two opposing sides, black and white
Complexity: Extremely high
Strategic Element: Very high
Game Duration: From several minutes to several days
Luck Component: No luck involved
Required Skills: Strategy, memory, observation, judgment, calculation

Overview:

According to legend, Go was invented around 2300 BC by Emperor Yao. His son, Danzhu, was proud and arrogant, prone to fits of anger and stubbornness. In order to temper his son's temperament, Emperor Yao invented Go.

In the game of Go, there are two types of stones, black and white. It is a rule that the player who holds the black stones makes the first move. Both players then take turns placing their stones on the intersections of the lines of a 19x19 grid board. Once a stone is placed, it cannot be moved, and players are not allowed to take back their moves. During the game, players aim to surround territory and capture opponent's stones. The winner is determined by the total area surrounded, or "territory", on the board.

The rules of Go are concise and elegant, yet the gameplay is infinitely varied. Mastery of its intricacies requires extensive practice and study. Chess grandmaster Emanuel Lasker praised it, saying, "If there are intelligent beings elsewhere in the universe, almost certainly they play Go." At the same time, Go is considered one of the most complex board games in the world. Its complexity was proven to be PSPACE-hard by Robertson and Munro in 1978.

As of mid-2008, there were over 40 million Go players worldwide, with the vast majority located in East Asia. By the end of 2020, the International Go Federation had a total of 77 member countries and 5 association members.

Modern Go:

After the Meiji Restoration, Go players lost support from the shogunate and began seeking new means of livelihood, leading to the emergence of newspaper Go matches and the modern ranking system, and the establishment of the national Japan Go Association. In the Showa era, Go players Wu Qingyuan and Kitani Minoru jointly ushered in the trend of the "new opening," marking the beginning of modern Go. Subsequently, top-tier Go players emerged in the Japanese Go world, such as Sakata Eio, Fujisawa Hideyuki, Takagawa Kaku, and later, Otake Hideo, Ishida Yoshio, Takeuchi Michio, Kobayashi Koichi, and others.

In 1989, Korean player Cho Hun-hyun won the inaugural Ing Cup World Go Championship, sparking a surge of interest in Go in Korea. Subsequently, numerous world Go tournaments emerged. Among these tournaments, Korean player Lee Chang-ho emerged as the dominant figure in the Go world at that time. After 2006, Lee Chang-ho's performance declined somewhat, and new forces such as Lee Se-dol and Gu Li began to challenge him. The following years saw a period of rivalry between Gu Li and Lee Se-dol.

In the 2010s, numerous Chinese Go players emerged prominently on the international stage. Players like Ke Jie, Fan Tingyu, Shi Yue, Mi Yuting, Chen Yao Ye, Zhou Ruiyang, and Tan Xiao, among others, consecutively won world championship titles.

Culture:

Throughout its millennia-long transmission, Go has gradually developed numerous terms. Some of these have entered mainstream vocabulary, such as "endgame." Nearly every move in Go is associated with a specific term, to the extent that one can vividly describe a local battle entirely in writing. For instance: "Black occupies the upper right corner's star point, White makes a small knight's move, Black makes a two-space high pincer, White makes a one-space approach move, Black follows with another approach move, White makes a small knight's move into the corner, Black blocks at the 3-3 point..." This passage vividly describes a common opening sequence using Go terminology.

Common terms include:

  • "Calling for capture": When a stone has only one liberty, it is called "calling for capture."
  • "Liberty": A liberty refers to an empty intersection point on the board that is adjacent to a stone. A single stone can have no more than four liberties, but a group of connected stones may have more than four liberties. When placing a stone threatens the liberties of opponent's stones, it's referred to as "tightening liberties" or "squeezing liberties."
  • "Capture": A stone with no liberties is captured and removed from the board. Capturing opponent's stones in this way is referred to as "capture."
  • "Eye": On the board, an empty intersection point surrounded by stones of one player is called an "eye."
  • "Territory": The sum of the number of eyes surrounded by living groups and the living groups themselves is referred to as "territory."
  • "Empty space": The area enclosed by stones on the board.
  • "Ko": A situation where both players can take turns capturing each other's stones. In the rules of Go, when a ko occurs, the player whose stone has been captured cannot directly recapture it but must first find a ko threat elsewhere, allowing them to recapture after their opponent responds to the threat.
